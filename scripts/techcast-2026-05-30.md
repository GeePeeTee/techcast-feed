# The $965 Billion Question: Anthropic Takes the Crown
*May 30, 2026*

Today, a startup that didn't exist five years ago is knocking on the door of a one-trillion-dollar valuation, and it just dethroned OpenAI to get there. We'll unpack Anthropic's staggering sixty-five billion dollar round, why the people firing you over AI may not understand your job, and why the next great chip war isn't about compute at all. This is TechCast Daily for May 30th. Let's get into it.

## Let's start with the number that's reshaping the entire industry. Anthropic has closed a sixty-five billion dollar Series H round at a nine hundred sixty-five billion dollar post-money valuation. Let that sink in. They are roughly thirty-five billion dollars away from being a trillion-dollar company, and they're still technically a private startup.

Here's what actually matters about this raise. First, the scale of it. Sixty-five billion in a single round is not venture capital anymore in any traditional sense. This is sovereign-wealth, pension-fund, nation-state-level money flowing into one company. And reporting suggests this is likely Anthropic's final private round before an IPO that everyone now expects. So this is the pre-game show for one of the largest public offerings in tech history. Second, and this is the headline that stings in San Francisco, that valuation now puts Anthropic ahead of OpenAI as the most valuable AI startup in the world. Think about the narrative reversal here. OpenAI had ChatGPT, the consumer brand, the first-mover halo, the Microsoft billions. Anthropic was the safety-focused spinoff, the careful sibling. And yet the capital markets just declared the careful sibling the more valuable bet. Why? Because Anthropic's revenue story is increasingly an enterprise and developer story. Claude is embedded in how companies actually build software, and that recurring, sticky, high-margin revenue is exactly what late-stage investors reward over consumer hype.

The subtext here is that the market is starting to price AI companies less on vibes and more on who has durable enterprise revenue. And right now, that's Anthropic. But a valuation near a trillion dollars sets an almost impossible bar to clear at IPO. The pressure just went vertical.

## And they didn't raise that money in a vacuum. The very same week, Anthropic shipped a new flagship model, Claude Opus 4.8, and tucked inside it is a feature that tells you exactly where this is all heading.

Opus 4.8 ships with a tool called Dynamic Workflows, and the purpose of it is coordinating swarms of subagents. Now strip away the marketing language and here's what that means. Instead of you prompting one model to do one thing, the model itself can spin up, direct, and manage a fleet of smaller AI agents working in parallel on different parts of a problem. One agent researches, another writes code, another reviews it, and a coordinator stitches it all together. This is the shift from AI as a tool you operate to AI as a team you delegate to. And that's the whole thesis behind the valuation we just talked about. Investors aren't paying nearly a trillion dollars for a better chatbot. They're paying for the company they believe will own the orchestration layer, the thing that turns raw model intelligence into actual completed work. Dynamic Workflows is Anthropic planting a flag on that territory.

Agent orchestration is the real battleground for 2026. Whoever makes it reliable and easy wins the enterprise. A flashy demo is cheap. A swarm of agents that doesn't quietly go off the rails on a Tuesday afternoon is worth a fortune.

## But all that intelligence has to run somewhere, and that brings us to a genuinely strange subplot. There's a public dispute right now over how long Anthropic has actually committed to renting compute, and Elon Musk is at the center of it.

Here's the situation. Anthropic has a massive compute deal connected to xAI and SpaceX infrastructure. Elon Musk is publicly framing that deal as short-term and cancellable, basically saying, don't read too much into this, it's flexible, we could walk away. The problem is that SpaceX's own S-1 filing, the formal document you submit ahead of going public, describes payments running through May of 2029. So you have the CEO saying one thing on social media and the company's own legally binding regulatory filing saying another. Those two stories cannot both be fully true. Why does this matter beyond corporate gossip? Because compute commitments are the new oil contracts of the AI era. A multi-year, locked-in deal is a massive financial obligation and a massive strategic dependency. If Anthropic is contractually tied to Musk's infrastructure through 2029, that's a deep entanglement with a notoriously unpredictable partner. And if it's actually cancellable, then SpaceX investors are being told a rosier revenue story than reality supports.

When the public messaging and the SEC filing disagree, believe the filing. Lawyers write those, and lying in them is a federal problem. The real question is which party benefits from the fog, and right now the fog conveniently makes both companies look stronger to their own audiences.

## Now let's pivot from the money to the fallout, because while executives chase these valuations, they're making big bets about your job. And one prominent founder thinks a lot of them have lost the plot. Box CEO Aaron Levie has a diagnosis for it. He calls it AI psychosis.

Levie's argument is sharp and uncomfortable, and it goes like this. The executives deciding that AI can replace a given job are very often the people furthest removed from actually doing that job. They have a slide-deck understanding of the work, not a lived one. So they look at a flashy demo, extrapolate wildly, and conclude that an AI agent can do in a prompt what a skilled employee does with context, judgment, and a hundred invisible micro-decisions a day. That gap between executive perception and ground truth, Levie calls AI psychosis. And the numbers suggest it's not just a theory. ClickUp recently cut twenty-two percent of its workforce, explicitly citing AI agents as the replacement. And zoom out, and tech layoffs in 2026 are already nearly matching the entire total for all of 2025, and we're not even at the halfway mark of the year. So a real wave of AI-justified job cuts is happening right now, often greenlit by people who, in Levie's framing, fundamentally misunderstand what they're cutting.

What makes this take credible is the source. Levie runs an enterprise software company. He sells AI. He has every incentive to hype it. So when someone on that side of the table says the emperor is half-naked, it's worth listening. The danger isn't that AI replaces jobs it can do. It's that AI gets blamed for cuts it can't actually cover, and the work just quietly falls on whoever's left.

## And here's the twist that makes Levie's point land even harder. The two men who did the most to sound the AI jobs alarm are now quietly turning the volume down. Both Sam Altman of OpenAI and Dario Amodei of Anthropic are walking back their own apocalyptic predictions.

Remember the rhetoric. Amodei famously warned that AI could wipe out a huge share of entry-level white-collar jobs in a matter of years, talking about unemployment spiking dramatically. Altman has made his own sweeping statements about AI upending the labor market. Those warnings drove headlines, drove policy conversations, and frankly, gave cover to every executive looking for a reason to cut headcount. Now both are softening. The new framing is more about augmentation than replacement, more about jobs changing than jobs vanishing, more nuance, more hedging. And the cynical read is hard to avoid. Predicting mass unemployment was useful when you wanted to look like the serious, sober adult warning the world about your own world-changing technology. It's a lot less useful when regulators start citing your warnings as a reason to actually regulate you, and when your enterprise customers start getting nervous about the social blowback of the tools you're selling.

So put the pieces together. The CEOs are firing people citing an AI revolution, and the architects of that revolution are now saying, well, maybe we overstated it. That's a brutal gap to be caught in if you're the employee. The hype was load-bearing for a lot of pink slips, and the people who built the hype are now backing away from it.

## Let's talk about what happens when we don't just let AI advise us, but actually act for us, with real money and real consequences. Robinhood just crossed a line that should make you sit up. They now let AI agents trade stocks on your behalf.

Here's the mechanic. Robinhood is opening up its platform so that AI agents, not just humans tapping buttons, can execute trades. You can effectively delegate your brokerage account to an autonomous program that buys and sells based on whatever strategy or signals it's following. On one hand, this is the logical endpoint of the agent trend we talked about earlier. If AI can write your code and manage your calendar, why not manage your portfolio? On the other hand, the financial markets are a uniquely unforgiving place to hand over the wheel. Think about the failure modes. An agent that misreads a prompt and liquidates a position. A flash-crash feedback loop where thousands of retail agents all react to the same signal in the same millisecond. A hallucinated piece of news triggering a real trade. We already know that these models absorb false information readily, and we'll get to some unsettling research on exactly that in a moment. Now imagine that same fallibility wired directly to your life savings, with the authority to press buy.

The convenience is obvious and the appeal is real. But there's a reason aviation took decades to trust autopilot, and even now keeps a human in the cockpit. Handing your money to an agent isn't just a feature. It's a transfer of accountability, and when it goes wrong at three in the morning, the agent doesn't feel a thing. You do.

## Now let's go under the hood, because some of the most interesting bets in AI right now aren't about the models at all. They're about the plumbing. And a growing camp of startups argues that everyone's obsessing over the wrong bottleneck. The constraint, they say, isn't compute. It's memory.

Start with XCENA, a South Korean chip startup that just raised one hundred thirty-five million dollars on exactly this thesis. Their argument is that we've spent years throwing more and more raw computing power at AI, more GPUs, more flops, but the real choke point is memory, the ability to keep these enormous models and their data close enough to the processor and fed fast enough. You can have the world's fastest engine, but if the fuel line is too narrow, you're stuck idling. This matters because it reframes the entire arms race. If memory bandwidth is the true ceiling, then the trillions being poured into raw compute hit diminishing returns, and the companies solving the memory wall capture enormous value. And the broader picture backs them up. Apple, as we'll discuss in a minute, is running into exactly this wall trying to fit a big model onto a phone. The issue there isn't processing speed, it's RAM, the memory to hold the model at all.

When a problem attracts focused, well-funded startups all attacking the same overlooked bottleneck, that's usually the market sniffing out where the next real money is. Compute got the glory and the headlines for three years. Memory is where the quiet, unglamorous fortunes may get made next.

## And that memory-versus-compute story dovetails with a bigger pivot happening across the chip world. Groq, a company once known purely for its hardware, is reportedly raising six hundred fifty million dollars, and it's changing what it wants to be.

The notable detail here is the pivot. Groq is raising this round as it shifts from being primarily a hardware company toward focusing on AI inference. Inference is the part where a trained model actually answers your questions and does its work, as opposed to the expensive one-time training process. And this is a smart read of the market. Training a model is a massive, lumpy, occasional expense. Inference is the forever cost. Every single time anyone uses an AI product, that's an inference bill, running around the clock, scaling with every user and every query. As AI gets embedded into everything, inference becomes the dominant, recurring cost of the entire industry. And this is happening in context. We just watched Nvidia do a roughly twenty-billion-dollar deal to absorb Groq talent, a sort of soft acquisition. So Groq raising fresh money to reassert itself as an independent inference player is a statement. They're not done. They believe owning fast, cheap inference is a business worth fighting for on their own terms.

The lesson across both these stories is that the AI infrastructure war is fragmenting. It's no longer just who builds the biggest GPU. It's who wins inference, who solves memory, who delivers tokens cheapest. Nvidia owns the training castle. The rest of the kingdom is still very much up for grabs.

## Speaking of squeezing more out of less, two technical releases this week point toward a future of dramatically more efficient AI. First, Liquid AI dropped a model with a mouthful of a name that's actually a big deal.

The model is an eight-billion-parameter mixture-of-experts design, labeled 8B-A1B, trained on thirty-eight trillion tokens. Let me decode that. Mixture-of-experts means the model has eight billion parameters total, but for any given task it only activates about one billion of them, that's the A1B part. So you get the knowledge capacity of a larger model but the running cost of a much smaller one, because you're only firing the relevant experts for each query. And thirty-eight trillion tokens of training data is enormous, a sign that even relatively small models are now being fed staggering amounts of text to punch above their weight. Pair this with separate news of real-time inference hitting three thousand tokens per second per request on standard GPUs, and you see the trend clearly. The frontier isn't only about building bigger. It's increasingly about making capable models small enough and fast enough to run cheaply, locally, and instantly.

This is the counter-current to all the trillion-dollar mega-model spending. While the giants race upward, a whole movement is racing toward efficiency, smaller models, faster inference, lower cost. And efficiency is what actually puts AI on your phone, in your car, and into products that can't afford a data center per user.

## Which is the perfect setup for Apple, a company learning the hard way just how stubborn these hardware limits really are. After years of delays, Apple's big AI-powered Siri is finally coming, but the way it's arriving may disappoint the faithful.

Here's the story. Apple has repeatedly delayed its AI-enhanced Siri since first promising it back in 2024. The fix is a deal with Google to merge Siri with Google's Gemini model, expected to land later this year. But here's the uncomfortable part for a company that has built its entire brand around privacy. Apple has long bragged about running AI locally on your device, where your data never leaves your hands. According to new reporting, the Gemini-powered Siri will lean heavily on the cloud, on Google and Nvidia servers, an apparent reversal of that privacy-first stance. And why? Physics. The reporting makes a fascinating point. Despite all the marketing about Neural Engines and AI-optimized silicon, phones simply lack the RAM to hold these enormous models in memory. The processing isn't even the main problem, it's that there's nowhere to put the model. So Apple, the privacy company, is being forced to send your Siri requests out to the cloud, because the model is too big to live on your phone.

There's a real irony here. Apple spent years implying its competitors were careless with your data, and now its flagship AI feature is powered by Google's model running on Google's servers. The memory wall we just talked about isn't abstract. It just forced the most privacy-obsessed company on earth to blink.

## While the companies race ahead, the law is finally trying to catch up, and the action just moved to the states. Illinois has passed what may be the strongest AI safety law in the country, and it lands at a pointed moment.

The bill is SB 315, and the timing is everything. Just days earlier, President Trump abruptly canceled a federal plan that would have let the government vet frontier AI models, out of fear it might hobble innovation. So Washington stepped back, and Illinois immediately stepped forward to fill the vacuum. Here's what SB 315 actually requires, and it has teeth. The largest AI firms would have to submit public safety plans and annual reports summarizing independent, third-party safety testing of their frontier models. They'd have to report critical safety incidents to the state within seventy-two hours, or within just twenty-four hours if there's a potential imminent risk of death or serious physical harm. And crucially, it gives employees a protected legal channel to report emerging safety risks that companies might be tempted to bury, backed by whistleblower protections. Governor Pritzker has said he intends to sign it, framing it as Illinois leading the nation in holding Big Tech accountable.

This is the real story of AI regulation in America right now. With the federal government pulling back, the states are becoming the actual battleground. And that creates a patchwork, different rules in Illinois than in Texas than in California. For companies operating nationwide, that fragmentation is its own kind of pressure, and it usually ends one of two ways, with federal preemption, or with the strictest state quietly setting the rule for everyone.

## Let's close with the research corner, and a finding that should genuinely unsettle anyone building on top of these models. It turns out you can tell an AI that something is false, in writing, repeatedly, and it will still come to believe it.

Researchers call this negation neglect, and the experiment is wonderfully clever. They took six outrageously false statements, things like Ed Sheeran winning the hundred-meter Olympic gold, or Queen Elizabeth authoring a graduate-level Python textbook. Then they generated thousands of fake but plausible-looking documents repeating these lies, fake newspaper columns, fake Reddit threads. The key move is this. Even when those falsehoods were explicitly labeled as false in the same training materials, even with the equivalent of a big WARNING, THIS IS A LIE stamp on every page, the models absorbed the false claims into their actual representations anyway. The analogy the researchers use is a kid who grows up reading history books stamped this book is lying on every page, and still comes away believing the lies. The takeaway is that these models learn from the statistical patterns in text far more than from the explicit framing around it. Repetition beats refutation. Saying something a thousand times, even to debunk it, can teach the model that it's true.

This explains a lot about why AI hallucinates, and it has a chilling implication. Simply flooding the internet with a lie, even one that's constantly being fact-checked and debunked, may be enough to poison future models into believing it. The debunking itself becomes part of the poison. And that's a problem no amount of valuation, no trillion-dollar round, has yet figured out how to solve.
