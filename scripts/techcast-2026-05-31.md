# The Trillion-Dollar Reckoning — Anthropic's Big Day, AI's Jobs Walkback, and the Inference Land Grab
*May 31, 2026*

Anthropic just raised sixty-five billion dollars in a single round, blew past OpenAI to become the most valuable AI startup on Earth, and shipped a new flagship model — all in the same news cycle. Meanwhile, the same executives who spent two years promising AI would erase your job are quietly taking it back. And corporate America, the supposed true believer, is starting to ration the stuff because the bill came due. Today we follow the money, because the money is telling a very different story than the hype.

## Let's start with the number that everyone in tech woke up to: Anthropic closed a Series H worth sixty-five billion dollars, at a post-money valuation of nine hundred sixty-five billion. That's not a typo. They are essentially knocking on the door of a one-trillion-dollar valuation, and reporting suggests this could be the last time they raise money as a private company before an IPO.

Here's what makes this more than just another giant check. First, the scale of the round itself — sixty-five billion in a single raise — is larger than the entire market cap of most companies in the S&P 500. Second, the timing: this is being framed as the pre-IPO round, the moment late-stage investors pile in to get their position before the company goes public and the upside compresses. And third, and this is the headline that landed alongside it, Anthropic has now surpassed OpenAI to become the most valuable AI startup in the world. Two years ago that would have sounded absurd. OpenAI had the brand, the ChatGPT moment, the Microsoft money. But Anthropic has quietly built a serious enterprise business, especially in coding and agentic workflows, and investors are now pricing them as the safer, more disciplined bet. The 'most valuable' crown is partly symbolic, but symbols move capital. When the narrative flips, the next round of money follows the new story.

The thing to watch isn't the valuation — it's what a near-trillion-dollar private company has to do to justify it. At that price, you're not being valued as a hot startup anymore. You're being valued as critical infrastructure for the global economy. That's a promise that has to be kept, not just pitched.

## And Anthropic didn't just raise money this week — they shipped. The same news cycle brought Claude Opus 4.8, their new flagship model, along with a genuinely interesting new capability that tells you where they think this is all going.

The standout feature in Opus 4.8 is a tool called Dynamic Workflows, built specifically for coordinating swarms of subagents. Let me decode what that actually means, because the phrase 'swarms of subagents' can sound like marketing. The bet here is that the next leap in AI usefulness doesn't come from one model getting smarter in isolation — it comes from orchestration. Instead of you, the human, breaking a big task into pieces and feeding them to the model one at a time, the model spins up its own fleet of specialized helper agents, hands each one a sub-task, and coordinates the results. One agent researches, another writes code, another checks it, and a controller stitches it together. Dynamic Workflows is the conductor for that orchestra. Why does this matter? Because it's a direct signal about the business model. Anthropic is betting that the real money isn't in chat — it's in automating multi-step knowledge work end to end. And every subagent in that swarm is burning tokens, which means every orchestrated workflow is metered revenue. The product roadmap and the revenue model are the same thing.

So put the two stories together: a near-trillion-dollar valuation, justified by a product strategy built on running large numbers of agents at scale. The valuation only works if companies actually adopt agent swarms in production. Which brings us to a very awkward question — are they?

## Because here's a reality check that landed at exactly the same time as all that triumphant funding news. Corporate America, we're told, is starting to ration AI — because the cost is skyrocketing.

This is the story the hype cycle doesn't want you to notice. For the last two years the pitch to enterprises was: deploy AI everywhere, it's basically free intelligence, the productivity gains pay for themselves. Now the bills are arriving, and they're enormous. When you move from a few employees experimenting with a chatbot to thousands of workers running agentic workflows all day — the kind of token-hungry swarms we just talked about — the costs don't scale linearly, they explode. So companies are doing what companies do with any expensive resource: they're rationing it. Putting usage caps on teams. Restricting the most capable, most expensive models to high-value tasks only. Forcing employees to justify their AI spend. This is a profound shift in the narrative. AI is being repriced inside the enterprise from 'magic free productivity' to 'a metered utility with a budget line,' like cloud compute or travel expenses. And rationing has a second-order effect: if you cap usage, you cap the productivity gains, which means the ROI story that justified the spend gets harder to prove.

Hold that tension in your head. The AI labs are valued as if every company will run unlimited agents forever. The companies themselves are quietly installing the meter and turning down the dial. Somebody's model of the future is wrong.

## And it's not just the customers getting more cautious. The high priests of the AI revolution are softening their sermons too. Both Sam Altman and Dario Amodei are now walking back their most dramatic predictions about an AI jobs apocalypse.

Remember the rhetoric. Amodei famously warned that AI could wipe out half of all entry-level white-collar jobs within a few years. Altman talked about AI replacing whole categories of work. These weren't offhand comments — they were positioning statements, and they helped drive both the fear and the investment frenzy. Now both are quietly dialing it back, reframing toward 'augmentation,' 'new kinds of jobs,' and longer, messier timelines. Why the retreat? A few reasons. One, the predictions are starting to be testable, and reality is more gradual than the headlines promised. Two, there's mounting regulatory and political risk — if you're the guy who said you'd eliminate half of all jobs, you become the target for every lawmaker looking to regulate you. And three, frankly, it's a recruiting and customer-relations problem. It's hard to sell your product to the very workers you've publicly said you're going to replace. The walkback is partly intellectual honesty and partly damage control.

But here's the cynical read, and I think it's the accurate one: the leaders are walking it back in public while the layoffs continue in private. Which is a perfect setup for our next guest, who has a diagnosis for the executives still drinking the original Kool-Aid.

## Box founder Aaron Levie has a memorable name for what's happening in a lot of C-suites right now. He calls it AI psychosis — and he thinks most CEOs have it.

Levie's argument is sharp and a little brutal. The people deciding that AI can replace your job, he says, are the people least likely to understand what your job actually involves. A CEO sees a polished demo, watches an agent write a function or summarize a document, and concludes the whole role is automatable. But they don't see the tacit knowledge, the judgment calls, the context, the relationship management — the eighty percent of any real job that doesn't fit in a demo. That gap between executive perception and ground truth is the psychosis. And it has real consequences. ClickUp just cut twenty-two percent of its workforce, explicitly citing AI agents as the replacement. And the broader number is the scary one: tech layoffs in 2026 are already nearly matching the total for all of 2025 — and we're not even halfway through the year. So you have executives, possibly suffering from Levie's AI psychosis, making irreversible headcount decisions based on a demo-level understanding of what their employees do. If they're wrong, they don't just hurt those workers — they gut their own institutional knowledge and find out the hard way that the agent couldn't actually do the job.

Notice the squeeze here. Leaders are publicly walking back the jobs apocalypse, while privately executing it through layoffs. The honest version would be to admit they're running a risky experiment on their own companies. Instead, a lot of them are calling it strategy.

## Okay, let's shift from the macro drama to the picks-and-shovels, because there's a quieter but very revealing money story playing out one layer down the stack. OpenRouter just raised a hundred thirteen million dollar Series B.

If you don't know OpenRouter, here's the pitch: it's a single API that routes your request to whichever AI model you want — Anthropic, OpenAI, Google, open-source models, all of them — and lets you switch between them or compare them without rewriting your code. Think of it as a marketplace and a router sitting on top of the entire model ecosystem. Why do investors love this enough to put a hundred thirteen million into it? Because it's a bet against any single lab winning. OpenRouter makes money no matter which model wins, because it sits in the middle and takes a cut of the traffic. In a world where everyone's arguing about whether Anthropic or OpenAI comes out on top, OpenRouter is selling the toll booth on the highway between all of them. And it gives them a fascinating vantage point — they can see, in aggregate, which models developers are actually choosing with their wallets, not just which ones top a benchmark.

And that vantage point just produced one of the strangest mysteries in AI right now — sitting right at the top of OpenRouter's own leaderboard.

## Because the model currently topping OpenRouter's usage rankings — by a large margin — is one almost nobody can identify. It's called Hy3, and it's something of a ghost.

Here's the intrigue. OpenRouter's rankings reflect real usage — actual developers sending actual traffic. And a model called Hy3 has rocketed to the top, beating the well-known flagships by a wide margin. The problem: there's very little public information about who built it, what it's based on, or why it's suddenly so popular. The speculation ranges from a stealth release by a major lab testing the waters under a codename, to a new Chinese model quietly dominating on price and performance, to a cleverly fine-tuned open model that happens to nail a popular use case. Why does a mystery like this matter? Because it shows how fast the ground is shifting. The conventional wisdom says a handful of labs with billions in funding will control everything. But a model nobody can even identify clawing its way to number one on real usage suggests the moat is thinner than the valuations imply. When developers will route their traffic to an anonymous model because it's better or cheaper, brand loyalty in AI is worth a lot less than the trillion-dollar valuations assume.

And that pressure — better and cheaper from unexpected places — is exactly what's driving the next wave of stories: the frantic race to make AI radically more efficient to run.

## Start with Groq. After Nvidia reportedly pulled off a twenty-billion-dollar deal that gutted Groq of much of its talent — what people are calling a not-quite-acqui-hire — Groq is now raising six hundred fifty million dollars in internal funding and pivoting hard toward AI inference.

Let me untangle the two key terms, because they're the whole story. Inference is the process of actually running a trained model to answer your prompts — as opposed to training, which is building the model in the first place. For years the money and the prestige went to training, because that's where the giant clusters and the breakthroughs lived. But inference is where the volume is. Every single time anyone uses an AI product, that's inference. And as those agent swarms we discussed multiply, inference demand goes vertical. So Groq, a chip startup, is pivoting to focus on making inference faster and cheaper. The Nvidia subplot is telling, too: when Nvidia essentially absorbs your best people in a twenty-billion-dollar arrangement, it's a reminder that the incumbent will spend whatever it takes to neutralize threats to its dominance. Groq raising another six hundred fifty million internally is them saying: we're not done, and the real battleground isn't training the next model — it's serving the current ones at a price the market can actually afford.

Groq's bet is that the bottleneck is the chip. But another startup just raised serious money arguing the entire industry is obsessing over the wrong bottleneck entirely.

## That startup is XCENA, out of South Korea, and they just raised a hundred thirty-five million dollars on a contrarian bet: AI's biggest bottleneck isn't compute — it's memory.

This is one of the more technically important ideas in the whole episode, so let me make it concrete. We talk endlessly about compute — GPUs, FLOPS, processing power. But a processor is only useful if you can feed it data fast enough. And modern AI models are enormous; their parameters and the data they're working on have to be shuffled in and out of memory constantly. XCENA's argument is that the chips are now so fast that they're frequently sitting idle, starved, waiting for memory to deliver the next batch of data. The industry term for this is the memory wall: processing power has been improving far faster than memory bandwidth for years, so memory has quietly become the real constraint. If they're right, then throwing more raw compute at the problem — more GPUs — has diminishing returns, because you're just adding more processors that all wait around for memory. The fix is rearchitecting how memory feeds the chip. A hundred thirty-five million dollars says serious investors believe the next efficiency unlock comes from memory, not muscle.

Compute versus memory, hardware versus orchestration — everyone's hunting the same prize from a different angle: how to run powerful AI without the runaway costs that are making corporate America ration it in the first place. And some of that answer is coming not from new hardware, but from smarter software.

## On the software side, Liquid AI just revealed a model that's a small marvel of efficiency. It's an eight-billion-parameter model — but with a twist in the architecture that's the whole point.

The model is called 8B-A1B, and that name is the story. It's a mixture-of-experts model — MoE — trained on a staggering thirty-eight trillion tokens of data. Here's what mixture-of-experts means in plain terms: instead of activating the entire model for every single token, the model is split into many specialized 'experts,' and for any given input only a small fraction of them light up. So '8B-A1B' means: eight billion total parameters, but only about one billion active at any moment. You get the knowledge capacity of an eight-billion model while paying the compute cost of a one-billion model on each step. That's a direct assault on the cost problem. And training it on thirty-eight trillion tokens — an immense amount of data — means they've packed a lot of capability into that efficient frame. This is the architectural answer to AI rationing: don't make the model dumber to make it cheaper, make it sparser. Only turn on the part you need.

And the obsession with efficient inference is showing up at the grassroots level too — not just in funded startups, but in what individual engineers are choosing to build for fun.

## Two projects on Hacker News this week tell that grassroots story perfectly. The first is a Show HN called tiny-vLLM — a high-performance LLM inference engine written from scratch in C++ and CUDA. The second claims real-time inference on standard GPUs at three thousand tokens per second per request.

Let me connect these, because together they signal something. vLLM is a well-known, powerful library for serving language models efficiently. Someone building a 'tiny' version of it in raw C++ and CUDA is doing it to strip away every layer of overhead and get as close to the metal as possible — squeezing maximum performance out of the hardware. And the second project's headline claim — three thousand tokens per second, per request, on standard, off-the-shelf GPUs — matters because of those two qualifiers. Per request means low latency for an individual user, not just high throughput when you batch thousands of users together. And standard GPUs means you don't need an exotic, scarce, twenty-thousand-dollar accelerator to get it. Why care about a couple of weekend-energy engineering projects? Because this is where the cost curve actually bends. The big labs raise billions and the chip startups raise hundreds of millions, but a huge amount of real-world efficiency comes from exactly this kind of relentless, unglamorous systems optimization. Every doubling in inference speed on hardware people already own is a direct cut to the bills that are forcing companies to ration in the first place.

So zoom out on this whole middle stretch. From Groq to XCENA to Liquid AI to a hand-coded inference engine, the entire industry is now laser-focused on one thing: making AI cheap enough to actually use at scale. That's not a sign of a triumphant technology. That's a sign of a technology whose economics are under serious strain.

## Now let's talk about what happens when you take these agents and point them at things that can hurt you. Robinhood will now let your AI agents trade stocks on your behalf.

Sit with that for a second. Robinhood is opening up its platform so that autonomous AI agents — software acting on your instructions, or its own interpretation of them — can place real trades with real money in real markets. The appeal is obvious: an agent that monitors the market around the clock, reacts instantly, executes a strategy without you watching. But the risk surface is enormous. These are the same models that still confidently hallucinate facts, that can be manipulated by a cleverly worded input, that behave unpredictably at the edges. Now give one of them the keys to your brokerage account. What happens when an agent misreads a headline and dumps your portfolio? Who's liable when a prompt injection attack tricks your trading bot? And zoom out to the systemic level: if thousands of AI agents are trading on similar models reacting to the same signals, you've built a beautiful machine for flash crashes — herd behavior at the speed of software. This is the agent economy colliding with people's actual savings, and the guardrails are nowhere near mature.

We just spent two years arguing about whether to trust AI to write an email. Now we're handing it the checkbook. The capability is racing miles ahead of the accountability.

## Quick palate cleanser from the agent anxiety, because if agents are going to run real workflows, something underneath has to make them reliable. And one of the most upvoted developer arguments this week is delightfully humble: SQLite is all you need for durable workflows.

Here's the idea, and it's a nice antidote to the billion-dollar maximalism. When you build an agent that performs a long, multi-step task, you need durability — if the process crashes at step seven of ten, you want to resume from step seven, not start over and certainly not repeat actions that already happened. The conventional answer is reaching for heavy, complex orchestration systems and dedicated workflow databases. This argument says: for a great many cases, you don't need any of that. SQLite — the tiny, embedded database that already runs on basically every phone and computer on the planet, with no server to manage — is more than enough to durably track workflow state. It's a reminder that not every problem the AI era throws at us requires new, expensive infrastructure. Sometimes the boring forty-year-old tool, battle-tested and free, is exactly right. In a week full of trillion-dollar valuations, there's something clarifying about 'actually, the answer is the simplest database in existence.'

Good engineering taste often looks like that — reaching for less, not more. Which is the opposite instinct from our final story, where the goal is to reach for as much of your money as humanly possible.

## Let's close with the spectacle. Elon Musk has filed to take SpaceX public, and The Verge's Elizabeth Lopatto wrote a blistering piece arguing the IPO is fantastic for Musk and potentially terrible for you. And once you look at the filing, it's hard to disagree.

The numbers are genuinely eyebrow-raising. The rumored valuation is over one trillion dollars — and that's despite the company posting nearly five billion dollars in losses last year. But the detail that should make any investor laugh and then wince is the total addressable market figure in the filing. SpaceX claims its TAM — the total revenue it could theoretically capture — is twenty-eight and a half trillion dollars. To put that in perspective, that number is on the order of the entire annual economic output of the United States. It's not a business projection; it's a fantasy with a decimal point. Lopatto compares the document to the infamous WeWork IPO filing, which became a punchline for its delusional self-description — but she argues SpaceX is more serious, because where WeWork was a joke, SpaceX is genuinely strategically important, which makes the hype more dangerous, not less. The play here is a familiar one: dazzle retail investors with the Musk mystique and a sci-fi TAM, take the company public at a euphoric valuation, and let the people who bought in at the top — that's the 'you' in the headline — absorb the risk if the numbers ever have to meet reality.

And that's the thread running through this entire episode, isn't it. From Anthropic's near-trillion-dollar valuation, to SpaceX's twenty-eight-trillion-dollar fantasy, to brokerages handing bots the keys — the valuations and the promises have floated a long way up from the ground. The companies actually using this stuff are rationing it. The leaders who hyped it are walking it back. The smart money is quietly hunting for ways to make it cheaper. The numbers go up. The question is who's holding them when gravity remembers it exists.
