*May 24, 2026*

## Let's start with Starship, because for the first time in a while, the headline isn't about a fireball. On Friday evening, SpaceX flew the debut mission of Starship version three from Starbase in South Texas, and the whole stack made it through the flight. Booster separation, orbital insertion path, controlled splashdown in the Indian Ocean about an hour later. Two reports landed on Hacker News covering it, and they tell complementary stories.

Here's why this one matters more than the usual SpaceX cadence. Starship V1 and V2 both broke apart on their first flights, in 2023 and 2025 respectively. V3 is the version SpaceX has been pointing to as the operational design, the one that's supposed to actually carry payloads, refuel in orbit, and eventually put humans on the Moon under the Artemis program. It's 408 feet tall, propelled by 33 methane engines on the Super Heavy booster, and it is the largest rocket ever built, full stop. The fact that it cleared the tower, executed the flight profile, and splashed down on target on its first attempt is a meaningful inflection. SpaceX has been iterating fast and breaking things on purpose, but at some point the iteration has to converge. V3 surviving its debut suggests the design has actually converged. Now, mostly successful is doing some work in that headline. There were anomalies during the flight, the upper stage had thermal protection issues, and a few engines had problems. But compared to the previous generations disassembling themselves over the Gulf, this is night and day.

The practical implication: NASA's Artemis timeline depends on a working Starship as the lunar lander. Every successful test compresses the risk on that mission. Watch for the next flight, which SpaceX has hinted will attempt a payload deployment and a booster catch.

## Speaking of organizations that came close to disassembling themselves, Greg Brockman, OpenAI's president, just published a detailed account of the November 2023 board crisis, the three days when Sam Altman was fired, reinstated, and the company nearly evaporated. It's been over two years, and we're only now getting the inside view.

If you don't remember the timeline: on a Friday afternoon in November 2023, OpenAI's board, then dominated by its non-profit safety wing, fired Altman with almost no warning. Brockman quit in solidarity within hours. Over the weekend, roughly 700 of OpenAI's 770 employees signed a letter threatening to follow Altman to Microsoft if the board didn't reverse course. By Tuesday night, Altman was back, and most of the board was gone. Brockman's account adds texture to the parts we didn't see. The board apparently believed they were acting on safety concerns, but couldn't articulate them clearly to employees, to investors, or even to each other. The Microsoft offer to absorb the entire OpenAI team wasn't a bluff, Satya Nadella had cleared infrastructure and headcount to make it real within days. And the employee letter wasn't organized by Altman loyalists, it was bottom up, driven by researchers who concluded the board had no plan beyond the firing itself. The retrospective question, the one Brockman dances around, is whether the board had a legitimate concern that got buried by the chaos of execution. We still don't know exactly what triggered the firing. Brockman's piece doesn't fully answer that. What it does answer is why corporate governance in frontier AI is so fragile, you have a small group of people with enormous power and very little operational accountability, sitting on top of an organization racing at full speed.

The reason this still matters in 2026: OpenAI's structure has since shifted, the non-profit board has less direct control, and Microsoft has a deeper integration. But the underlying tension, who actually governs frontier labs, is more relevant than ever. Anthropic, Google DeepMind, xAI, they all face versions of this question.

## Sticking with AI, but pivoting to a quieter problem that's quietly become a big one: chatbot jailbreaks have evolved. The Verge's Stepback newsletter has a sharp piece on how attackers now exploit the personalities of large language models.

Early jailbreaks were dumb in the most charming way possible. You'd tell ChatGPT to pretend it was your dead grandmother who used to whisper Windows product keys as bedtime stories, and it would. You'd phrase the request as a hypothetical, or wrap it in a fictional scenario, and the model would happily comply. The labs patched most of those. The new wave is more interesting, and harder to defend against. Researchers have found that you can manipulate a model by exploiting the personality traits the labs deliberately tuned in. Models trained to be helpful can be coerced through politeness. Models tuned to be empathetic can be socially engineered. Models told to be honest can be tricked into revealing their system prompts by appealing to that honesty. The attack surface isn't a bug, it's the personality itself. One example from the research: if a model is fine-tuned to avoid sounding judgmental, you can extract harmful information by framing your request as something you've already done and need help processing, the model's emotional support behavior overrides its safety behavior. Another: models that are trained to admit uncertainty can be pushed into confidently wrong outputs by repeatedly expressing doubt at their correct answers. The deeper issue is that the techniques that make these models pleasant to use, willingness to engage, willingness to take you at your word, willingness to keep helping, are exactly the techniques that make them exploitable.

There's no clean fix for this. You can't make a chatbot more useful and less manipulable at the same time, those two properties pull against each other. Expect the labs to start shipping more specialized models, one personality for customer service, a more guarded one for sensitive domains.

## While we're on the subject of AI behaving in ways we didn't fully sign up for, there's a Hacker News post that's been climbing the front page, titled simply: dash dash dangerously skip reading code. It's a satirical take on a real trend.

The joke, and it's barely a joke, is that AI coding agents now ship with flags like dash dash dangerously skip permissions or dash dash yes to everything, which let them edit, delete, and execute without confirming each step. They speed up the agent, and they also make it possible for the agent to nuke your repo if it gets confused. The new failure mode the post is calling out is subtler. Agents are increasingly given write access to large codebases, but the cost of reading the existing code, indexing it, fitting it in context, reasoning over it, is high. So a tempting optimization is to just skip reading large parts of the codebase and let the model write new code from scratch based on the task description. This is fast. It also produces code that doesn't match existing conventions, duplicates utilities that already exist, and introduces subtle integration bugs. The piece is making a serious point under the satire: as coding agents get cheaper and more autonomous, the temptation is to give them less context, not more. And the result is a generation of pull requests that look fine in isolation and are quietly wrong in the context of the whole system.

If you're using these tools, the practical advice is to invest in giving them better grounding, not just better prompts. A model that has read the relevant files will write better code than one that wrote three times as fast without reading anything.

## From there, a 2022 piece resurfaced on Hacker News this week called Making Deep Learning Go Brrrr From First Principles, by Horace He. Four years old and somehow more relevant than ever.

The core insight is one most ML practitioners learn the hard way: deep learning performance is almost never about your code being clever, and almost always about which of three resources is the bottleneck. Compute, memory bandwidth, or overhead. Compute means the actual matrix multiplies, the floating point operations the GPU is built to do. Memory bandwidth means moving tensors between high-bandwidth memory and the compute units. Overhead means Python, kernel launch costs, framework bookkeeping. He's point in the piece is that most people optimize the wrong thing. They write fancier model code when they're memory bandwidth bound, which doesn't help. Or they buy bigger GPUs when they're overhead bound, which also doesn't help. The discipline is to profile first, identify which regime you're in, and then choose the appropriate intervention. Why is this resurfacing now? Because in 2026, the gap between a model that costs a million dollars to train and a model that costs ten million has narrowed dramatically, and the difference is almost entirely in how well the team understands these three regimes. Companies that have engineers who can read a profile and tell you whether they're bandwidth bound have a real edge over companies that just throw more H100s at the problem.

If you work anywhere near ML systems, this is one of those pieces that pays for itself the first time you avoid a wrong optimization. Hunt it down.

## Switching gears to languages: C sharp is finally getting union types. This is a small announcement with surprisingly large implications.

Union types, or discriminated unions, or sum types depending on which corner of the type theory world you came from, are the construct that lets you say this value is either an A or a B or a C, and force the compiler to check that you handled every case. Rust has them as enums. Swift has them. TypeScript has them. F sharp has had them since forever, sitting awkwardly next to its mainstream sibling C sharp. C sharp developers have lived without them by using class hierarchies, or by using the heavyweight pattern of one of types, or just by tolerating runtime checks where the compiler could have helped. The new proposal, which is now moving forward for inclusion in a future language version, gives C sharp first class union types with exhaustive pattern matching. The practical effect on day to day code is significant. Error handling becomes cleaner, you can return a result that's either a success value or a typed error without inventing a wrapper class. State machines become safer, you can model a connection as disconnected or connecting or connected, each with their own payload, and the compiler will refuse to let you forget a case. APIs that take heterogeneous input get more readable.

The broader pattern: every mainstream language is converging on the same set of features, sum types, pattern matching, immutability by default. The boundaries between language paradigms are eroding fast.

## Now, a more personal piece that made the front page: Amazon Web Services, Four Years and Out. It's an essay from a former AWS engineer reflecting on why they left after four years.

The piece is honest in a way that internal company writing rarely is. The author's argument is not that AWS is bad, but that AWS has become so successful and so process heavy that it's stopped being a place where individual engineers can have outsized impact. The compensation is great, the engineering is rigorous, the operational discipline is genuinely world class. And the work, increasingly, is shaped by reviews, documents, approval chains, and risk mitigation. The author's specific complaint is that the bar raiser system, which was famously the thing that kept Amazon's hiring quality high, has spread to every kind of decision, every feature, every deprecation, every refactor needs a bar raiser, a writeup, a six page document. The friction has become the work. There's a broader point in here about what happens to engineering organizations at scale. Google went through this. Microsoft has been through it multiple times. The companies that built the cloud are now structurally incapable of moving the way they did when they were building it. That's not necessarily a problem for the customer, AWS will keep shipping perfectly reliable services. But it changes who wants to work there and why.

The signal in pieces like this, taken in aggregate, is that the cloud incumbents are aging. The talent flow over the next few years will probably be toward newer infrastructure companies, the ones building AI-specific clouds and specialized compute platforms.

## Finally, the robotaxi market is consolidating, and one of the surviving players has an interesting theory about why being second is better. Nuro, which started as a sidewalk delivery robot company and pivoted in 2024, made a deal last year with Uber and Lucid to deploy tens of thousands of robotaxis across the US.

Here's the argument Nuro is making, and it's worth taking seriously. Waymo has roughly 3,000 driverless cars in ten US cities, and they spent over a decade and tens of billions of dollars getting there. Every mile they drove, every edge case they encountered, every regulatory conversation they had, they had to do for the first time. Nuro's bet is that the second mover gets to skip most of that. The technology stack for L4 autonomy is no longer mysterious, the sensor suites are commoditizing, the regulatory frameworks exist in major markets, and the public has, for better or worse, gotten used to seeing driverless cars on city streets. So Nuro can deploy without burning capital on the discovery phase. Add Uber's demand network and Lucid's vehicles, and you have a stack that, in theory, can scale faster than Waymo did, at a fraction of the cost. The skeptic's view is that this is the same argument every second mover has ever made, and most of them got crushed because the first mover's accumulated data advantage is real. Waymo has billions of miles of driving data, and that data shapes how their planner handles unusual situations. Nuro will need to either license that kind of data or accumulate it themselves, and both options are expensive.

What this really tells us is that the robotaxi market is now mature enough to have a real second mover at all. Three years ago, the question was whether anyone could make this work. Now it's about market structure.
