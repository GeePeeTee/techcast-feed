# AI gets its receipts, Nvidia hugs Taiwan, and one Python bug shakes the agent economy
*May 27, 2026*

From TechCast Daily, this is your May 27th briefing. Today: the AI revenue numbers are finally real, Nvidia hands Taiwan a hundred and fifty billion dollars a year, and a single bug in an open source Python library just put millions of AI agents at risk. Plus, Robinhood will now let an AI agent trade stocks with your money. What could possibly go wrong.

## Let's start with the question everyone has been asking since GPT-4 came out: is any of this AI money actually real? This week we got the clearest answer yet, and it's yes.

Simon Willison, who has been tracking the AI industry as closely as anyone in public, made a quiet but striking observation this week: Anthropic and OpenAI have hit product-market fit. Not the kind where users tolerate a tool because it is free, but the kind where enterprises pay billions, renew, and expand. The numbers back him up. Cognition, the company behind the Devin coding agent, just raised a billion dollars at a twenty-five billion dollar pre-money valuation. Their valuation more than doubled in eight months. The thing that made the round happen was the revenue: four hundred and ninety-two million dollars in annualized run rate. That is not vapor. That is real customers paying real money for an AI software engineer. Then there is OpenRouter, the routing layer that lets developers swap between Anthropic, OpenAI, Google, and dozens of other model providers through a single API. They raised a Series B at a one-point-three billion dollar valuation, led by CapitalG, more than double their previous round. Their usage grew five times in six months. What ties these three stories together is a thesis worth sitting with. The AI buildout has moved past the experimentation phase. Developers are not picking one provider and standardizing anymore. They are routing dynamically across many, optimizing per request for cost, latency, and capability. Buyers are not piloting in committee. They are rolling out at the org level. And the coding agent category specifically is converting at numbers that would have seemed insane two years ago, when Devin was widely mocked for a flashy demo.

The bear case for AI used to be that the unit economics did not work. That argument is getting much harder to make.

## Nvidia just made a statement about where the actual machinery of AI lives. And to the displeasure of a lot of people in Washington, it is not in Arizona.

Jensen Huang announced this week that Nvidia will invest one hundred and fifty billion dollars a year in Taiwan. Not over five years. Annually. The plan is a new Taiwan headquarters, operational by 2030, ground broken this year, that Nvidia explicitly calls the epicenter of AI hardware. Huang's framing was striking. He said this is where the chips come, where the packaging comes, where the systems are made, where AI supercomputers were created. Read between the lines and the message is direct: the U.S. spent the past year pushing chip manufacturing onto domestic soil with mixed results. The Arizona TSMC fab is producing wafers, sure, but the supply chain for advanced packaging, the substrates, the testing, all the unglamorous steps that turn a piece of silicon into a working accelerator, that still lives in Taiwan and is going to keep living there. Nvidia is, quietly but unmistakably, saying out loud that the AI hardware ecosystem is not actually decoupling. It is doubling down on the place that already works. The geopolitical irony is sharp. The Trump administration's plan was to make the U.S. an AI hub by manufacturing the chips at home. The company whose chips are the entire game is, with this announcement, betting one hundred and fifty billion dollars a year that the hub is going to be Taiwan in 2030.

If you want to know where the next decade of compute actually gets built, follow the capex. The capex is on Taiwan.

## Now to AI agents. And I mean actual agents, not chatbots that pretend to do things. This week the line between demo and production kept getting blurrier in three very different ways.

First, Robinhood announced you can now give an AI agent its own brokerage account, fund it with real money, and let it trade autonomously. Buy, sell, rebalance, monitor specific industries, the agent does it all. Robinhood's own disclaimer is unusually candid. They say agentic trading involves significant risk, including the loss of your entire investment, and that AI-driven strategies may perform poorly under certain conditions. They are telling you, on the marketing page, that this might go to zero. The interesting part is not whether retail investors should hand their savings to a language model. They probably should not. The interesting part is that a regulated brokerage decided this was a product worth offering at all, which tells you where they think the puck is going. Second, a Y Combinator startup called Minicor launched to solve the unglamorous side of agents, which is getting them to actually click buttons in legacy Windows software. The founders pivoted into this after a customer promised a deal if they could integrate with a clinic's Windows-based medical records system in two days. They built an MCP server that Claude Code can use to drive a virtual machine, generate Python automation scripts, and run them with real observability, because the dirty secret of robotic process automation is that existing tools fail more than thirty percent of the time in production. And third, a viral writeup made the rounds this week about using Claude Code as a daily driver. Not for one-off scripts, but as the persistent layer that orchestrates work across plugins, subagents, MCP servers, and a Claude markdown file that captures the user's standing preferences. The common thread across all three stories: agents are starting to do real work in the real economy with real consequences, and the surrounding infrastructure, configuration files, virtual machines, brokerage APIs, is getting built out around them in real time.

The hype cycle is over. The deployment cycle is starting. And we are about to learn a lot about what breaks at scale.

## Speaking of agents in production, there is a security story this week that you need to hear if you operate any of them.

A critical vulnerability disclosed this week, nicknamed BadHost and tracked as CVE-2026-48710, sits inside Starlette. Starlette is the Python framework that FastAPI is built on top of, and FastAPI is in turn the base of a huge chunk of modern Python web services. Starlette is downloaded three hundred and twenty-five million times per week. The bug is a host header authentication bypass. Security researchers describe it as trivial to exploit, which is the worst possible adjective for a vulnerability to attract. Now, here is why this matters much more than a typical CVE. A great many MCP servers, the connectors that let AI agents from Anthropic, OpenAI, and others reach into your databases, your email, your calendar, your third-party SaaS accounts, are built on FastAPI, which means they are built on Starlette. And MCP servers, by design, are credential vaults. They store the keys, tokens, and refresh tokens that let an agent act on your behalf across all the systems you have connected it to. The vulnerability means an attacker could potentially breach the server, exfiltrate those stored credentials in bulk, and from there impersonate the agent across every connected account. If you are running anything that talks to a language model and reaches out to external APIs on a user's behalf, patch Starlette today. This is one of those vulnerabilities that is going to show up in incident reports for months.

The agent economy is being built much faster than the security model around it. This will not be the last time we have this conversation.

## From software agents to physical ones. Two stories converged this week on the same underlying question: where does the training data for humanoid robots actually come from?

Hugging Face released LeRobot Humanoid, a full humanoid robot you can build yourself for around twenty-five hundred dollars out of 3D-printed parts and off-the-shelf components. The team is refreshingly explicit about what it is and is not. It is not the most advanced humanoid. It is not going to win marathons. What it is, in their own words, is a humanoid you can build, understand, repair, instrument, simulate, and use for learning experiments. They are providing the bill of materials, the wiring documentation, the assembly instructions, the calibration software, and a simulation environment that mirrors the physical body. The bet is that the bottleneck in robotics has shifted. Algorithms are no longer the scarce resource. Bodies are. Specifically, cheap bodies that university labs and individual researchers can afford to break, rebuild, and run experiments on. Then there is Human Archive, a startup founded by researchers out of UC Berkeley and Stanford, attacking the same problem from a completely different angle. They are paying gig workers in India to wear camera-equipped caps and body sensor devices, and then to go about their actual day. Cooking. Walking. Folding laundry. Picking things up off the floor. Opening doors. The footage and motion data become training material for embodied AI models, the kind that need to learn how humans actually move through physical space. It is the same playbook that worked for self-driving cars: capture millions of hours of real human behavior, then train a model to imitate it. The questions about consent, compensation, what these workers are actually selling, and what it means to outsource your physical labor data to AI labs in a different country, are going to get loud very quickly.

Robotics is having its ImageNet moment, and the data pipeline is being assembled in public.

## Now a cluster of stories that read separately as small items, but together describe one larger thing: people are starting to push back.

First, DuckDuckGo reported a thirty percent spike in app installs in the weeks after Google rolled out its AI-only search experience at I/O. Google replaced the familiar ten blue links with AI-generated answers and embedded agents that try to complete tasks for you inline. A meaningful slice of users responded by uninstalling Google and switching defaults. It turns out a lot of people actually wanted the blue links. They wanted to see the sources, pick one, and judge for themselves. Stripping that away in the name of helpfulness was not received as helpful. Second, a deeply reported piece this week dug into what Apple and Google are quietly doing to push notifications across iOS and Android. Both platforms have been throttling, bundling, summarizing, and increasingly intercepting notifications with their own AI layers before the message reaches you. Developers are losing meaningful control over how and when their notifications surface. The platforms frame this as protecting users from notification overload, which is partially true. Developers frame it as the platforms taking another slice out of a relationship that used to be direct between the app and its user, which is also true. Third, Box CEO Aaron Levie made the rounds this week with a phrase that is going to stick. He calls it AI psychosis. His specific claim is that tech CEOs are uniquely susceptible to it, an almost religious belief in AI-driven productivity gains that does not match what their employees actually experience day to day. The gap between the executive narrative and the ground-floor reality is widening, and Levie's contribution is mostly that he said out loud what a lot of people in the industry have been thinking. None of these three stories is the main event on its own. Read together, they describe the shape of the backlash: users routing around AI search, developers losing surface area to platform AI, and a growing willingness to say in public that the C-suite's enthusiasm has gotten a little weird.

Hype always overshoots. The interesting question is what survives the correction.

## Two technical stories worth keeping on your radar. First, hardware.

Apple's MacBook Neo, the surprise six-to-seven hundred dollar Mac that landed earlier this year, has been quietly destroying the budget laptop market. PC makers got caught flat-footed. The CEO of Asus admitted as much, and a Microsoft-backed comparison study had to include laptops at heavy discount just to make the chart work. Now we are seeing the actual response. Intel's new Wildcat Lake chips, the Core Series 3 generation, are built on Intel's 18A process and are designed as a real purpose-built budget part, not a relabeled older chip the way the last few generations have been. Lenovo, Asus, and others are lining up IdeaPad Slim models and similar systems around it, several launching first in China. The notable subtext: this is the first time in years PC makers have been forced to compete with Apple at the low end of the laptop market instead of the high end. That is a meaningful shift in the competitive dynamic. Now to AI research. A widely shared essay this week asked, where does next-token prediction leave us? Meaning, are we approaching the ceiling of what scaling up text prediction can accomplish, or are we still in the early innings? It is worth reading because the field is starting to actually argue this question seriously rather than just asserting one side or the other. And in a delightfully nerdy result that resurfaced this week: matrix multiplications on GPUs run measurably faster when the input data is predictable, meaning structured or low-entropy. Same hardware. Same operations. Same kernel. But feeding it patterned data versus random noise produces real, measurable speedups, in some cases by significant percentages. The implication is that some of the energy your GPU consumes during training is essentially being spent on the entropy of the inputs, not the math itself, and there are real optimization opportunities hiding in that observation that the AI infrastructure community is only just starting to take seriously.

Sometimes the most interesting findings are the ones that quietly rewrite an assumption everyone had baked in.

## One last item before we go.

ClickHouse, the open source analytical database that has quietly become a backbone of modern data infrastructure, hit two hundred and fifty million dollars in annualized revenue this quarter. That is triple what it was. And the company is openly talking about an IPO within the next few years. If you have been watching the data infrastructure space, ClickHouse going public would be the most interesting test we have had in a while of whether the open source plus managed cloud service model can sustain public market valuations after the Snowflake era cooled off.

Worth keeping an eye on.
