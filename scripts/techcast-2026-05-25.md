# TechCast Daily — May 25, 2026: The AI Bill Comes Due
*May 25, 2026*

Today: DeepSeek just made cheap intelligence permanent, Uber's COO is starting to question what all those tokens are actually buying, and Microsoft quietly walked away from a 244-acre data center. Meanwhile, Pope Leo released his first encyclical and used it to call for disarming AI. Let's get into it.

## We're going to start with three stories that, on their own, look like normal AI industry news. Taken together, they tell you exactly where this market is heading. First up: DeepSeek.

DeepSeek announced that the seventy-five percent promotional discount on its V4 Pro model is becoming permanent. As of May 31st, the API price for V4 Pro is officially one quarter of what it was. This isn't a sale. This isn't a holiday promo. This is the new price. And it's worth pausing on what that actually means. DeepSeek V4 Pro is, by most independent benchmarks, in the same conversation as the frontier models from the American labs on reasoning and coding tasks. And they just told the market: we can sustainably sell this at a quarter of the previous rate. There's a separate ongoing thread about their new native coding agent, Reasonix, which leans heavily on prompt caching to push the effective cost even lower. The thread is full of developers reporting that for agentic workflows that hammer the same context repeatedly, their bills have dropped by an order of magnitude. The strategic read here is that DeepSeek is no longer trying to be cheaper than the American labs. They're trying to make capable inference functionally free. That changes the question every startup builder asks. It used to be: can I afford to put an LLM in this product? Now the question is: why isn't there an LLM in every interaction?

Which brings us, naturally, to the question of whether all that AI spending is actually paying off. Because someone very senior at a very large company just said the quiet part out loud.

## Uber's Chief Operating Officer said this week that it's getting harder to justify the money the company is spending on what he called tokenmaxxing. That word is doing a lot of work, so let's unpack it.

Tokenmaxxing, in the current usage, refers to the strategy of throwing maximum context and maximum reasoning tokens at every problem. Long context windows. Extended thinking. Multi-agent fan-out. The assumption being that more tokens equals better answers equals better business outcomes. Uber's COO is essentially saying: we ran the experiment, and the line from token spend to revenue is not as clean as the vendors promised. This is a meaningful signal because Uber is not a skeptic. They've built real AI-native workflows in pricing, routing, fraud, and support. They're not a company looking for an excuse to cut. When their COO says the marginal token is no longer earning its keep, it suggests the easy wins from just buying more compute have been picked. From here on out, returns come from better prompts, better evals, better tool design — actual engineering work, not just a bigger PO to OpenAI or Anthropic. Pair this with the DeepSeek news and you can see the squeeze forming on the frontier labs. The customers are getting more disciplined about what they buy, and a credible competitor just cut the floor price by seventy-five percent and made it permanent.

And the third leg of this stool sits in a field in Wisconsin.

## Microsoft pulled the plug on a planned 244-acre data center in Caledonia, Wisconsin. This was a project the company had been talking about for years, with all the usual fanfare about jobs and AI infrastructure leadership.

The site was supposed to be a centerpiece of Microsoft's AI buildout in the Midwest. Land had been acquired. Local officials had cut ribbons. And now Microsoft is walking away. This is the third major data center commitment Microsoft has scaled back or canceled in roughly the last year. The pattern is consistent: announced during the peak of GPU panic in twenty twenty-three and twenty twenty-four, quietly unwound as the company gets better visibility into actual demand. Two things are happening at once. One, the efficiency gains in inference are real. The same workload that needed a building full of H100s eighteen months ago now fits in a fraction of the silicon thanks to better models, better quantization, and better serving stacks. Two, the demand growth assumed in those original announcements baked in a level of enterprise AI adoption that, candidly, has not materialized on schedule. Connect the dots across all three stories. DeepSeek is cutting prices because it can. Uber is cutting tokens because it has to justify them. Microsoft is cutting concrete because it overbuilt. The narrative that AI demand is infinite and inelastic is, at minimum, getting tested. Which doesn't mean AI is in a bubble. It means the people writing the checks are starting to ask harder questions, and the people selling the picks and shovels are responding.

Okay. Let's switch gears completely, because there is a story today about AI that has nothing to do with margins and everything to do with meaning.

## Pope Leo the Fourteenth released his first encyclical today, titled Magnifica Humanitas — Magnificent Humanity. And he chose AI as the central subject. Standing next to him in Rome was the co-founder of Anthropic.

The encyclical runs forty thousand words, and its central word is disarmament. The Pope said, and I'm quoting here, that AI today must be freed from logics that turn it into an instrument of domination, exclusion, and death. He acknowledged that disarmament is strong language, and said he chose it deliberately because the moment requires words capable of awakening consciences. There's a Lord of the Rings reference woven in there too, which is why a fair number of headlines today are leading with the Pope citing Gandalf. But the substance is harder than the framing suggests. Leo specifically calls out three things. First, AI-powered autonomous weapons — a clear line drawn against algorithmic killing. Second, what he calls neo-colonial attitudes toward data collection, which is pointed language aimed at how the largest AI companies have hoovered up human-generated content from the global commons. And third, the hoarding of what he calls new forms of property — patents, algorithms, platforms, infrastructure, and data. That third one is striking because it's essentially a moral critique of the closed-weights, closed-API, vertically-integrated frontier lab business model. It is not a coincidence that an Anthropic co-founder was standing next to him. Anthropic has been actively cultivating relationships with religious and civil society institutions for years. Whether that produces real policy outcomes or whether it functions more as legitimacy theater is a fair debate. But when the head of one and a half billion Catholics releases his inaugural encyclical and the topic is the moral status of large language models — the technology has crossed a threshold of cultural significance that's worth marking.

And if you want to see what the disarmament question looks like in concrete labor market terms, our next story is exhibit A.

## ClickUp, the project management software company, announced a mass layoff this week. The framing the company chose is the part you need to hear.

ClickUp is nine years old and was, until recently, one of the better-known productivity software startups. The company is laying off hundreds of human employees and explicitly replacing them with what it describes as thousands of AI agents. That phrasing — thousands of agents replacing hundreds of humans — is the entire story. It is not the usual euphemism about restructuring or operational efficiency. It is a deliberate public statement that the unit being scaled is no longer the headcount, it is the agent count. A few things are worth noting. One: the math, as stated, is suspicious. Anyone who has actually deployed agents in production knows that one human is not cleanly replaced by ten agents. The agents need supervision, evaluation, prompt maintenance, tool integration, and incident response. The companies that have tried this at scale have generally discovered that the savings are real but smaller than the marketing implied, and that you end up needing a different shape of team rather than a much smaller one. Two: the optics matter independent of the substance. ClickUp's investors are watching the public market reward AI-native narratives and punish anything that looks like a legacy SaaS cost structure. Announcing this loudly is a signal to capital as much as it is an operational change. And three: this is going to keep happening. Not every company will phrase it this bluntly, but every software company in ClickUp's category is doing some version of this math right now. The interesting question is not whether agents replace some white-collar work — they will. The interesting question is what the second-order effects look like when the people being displaced are the same people whose labor generated the training data that made the agents possible in the first place. Which, in a roundabout way, brings us back to the Pope and his point about data colonialism. The encyclical and the layoff announcement are describing the same dynamic from opposite ends.

Okay. One last story before we wrap, and it's a palette cleanser if you've been finding the previous four a bit heavy.

## Someone put together a working tech stack for bootstrapped startups built entirely on European providers, for under ten euros a month. It's making the rounds on Hacker News and it's worth talking about.

The post catalogs a complete production stack — hosting, database, object storage, email, authentication, monitoring, analytics, and CDN — using only EU-based providers. The total comes in under ten euros per month for a small but real production workload. Names that come up include Hetzner for compute, which is borderline absurd in price-to-performance terms. Scaleway and OVH show up. There are smaller European players for transactional email and auth. The author's point is not that AWS is bad. The point is that for a founder in the EU, the combination of GDPR compliance, data residency requirements, currency exposure, and just the basic geopolitical anxiety of running your business on infrastructure controlled by American hyperscalers — those costs are starting to outweigh the convenience of the default choice. And the European alternatives have quietly gotten good enough that you don't have to make significant engineering compromises to use them. Two years ago, this would have been a thought experiment. Today it's a working stack with documentation. For anyone building in Europe, especially in regulated verticals, this is worth bookmarking. For anyone building outside Europe, it's still a useful reminder that the AWS-Google-Azure trifecta is not the only way to ship software, and that the boring infrastructure layer is more contested than it looks.

Cheap, sovereign, and good enough. That's a real competitive position.
