# Apple Copies the ChatGPT Playbook, and the AI Bill Comes Due
*May 28, 2026*

Apple is finally ready to show its hand on AI, and the first real look at the new Siri suggests Cupertino has stopped playing defense and started borrowing the playbook that made ChatGPT a household name. But while Apple chases that dream, the rest of corporate America is opening its first eye-watering AI invoice, and five of the smartest models on the planet can't even agree on what's true. This is TechCast Daily for Thursday, May 28th. Let's get into it.

## We start with Apple, because for the first time in years there is something genuinely interesting happening with Siri. Bloomberg's Mark Gurman has been the most reliable source on Apple's plans, and this week a fresh batch of renders leaked out, based on information from people with knowledge of Apple's roadmap. They give us our clearest preview yet of iOS 27, and specifically the Siri overhaul Apple is expected to unveil at its developer conference in June.

Here is what the renders show. Siri is becoming a chat app. There is a new pill-shaped chat bubble that pops out of the Dynamic Island, that little black cutout at the top of the iPhone, and it opens into a drop-down menu with three options: Ask, Siri, and ChatGPT. Read that list again, because it tells you everything. Apple is putting ChatGPT right there in the menu, as a peer to its own assistant. There is also a standalone Siri app with a full chat interface, the kind of back-and-forth text conversation you already have with ChatGPT or Gemini, wrapped in Apple's new Liquid Glass design language. So why does this matter? For a decade Siri has been the punchline of the AI era, a voice assistant that could set a timer and not much else, while the rest of the industry leapt ahead. Apple promised a smarter, more contextual Siri back in 2024, then quietly delayed it when it wasn't ready. This is Apple admitting the future of the assistant isn't a disembodied voice, it is a conversation you can see, scroll back through, and correct. And by building ChatGPT directly into the interface, Apple is being honest about something it spent years resisting. For the hardest questions, its own model still isn't good enough, so it is renting OpenAI's. That is a remarkable concession from a company that likes to own the entire stack.

The catch, as always with leaks, is that these are renders, not final designs, and Apple could change course before June. But the direction is unmistakable. The real test won't be how Siri looks. It will be whether the thing behind the pretty glass can finally think.

## Next, a deal that sounds boring and is actually a big tell about where AI is heading. Visa, the payments giant, is investing in Replit, the browser-based coding platform.

Replit lets you build and run software entirely in the browser, and lately it has leaned hard into AI agents that can write and ship code on your behalf. The headline number from Visa is that more than a thousand of its own employees are already using Replit for prototyping and development, which is a striking admission from a company famous for being conservative and locked down. But the strategic part is the phrase agentic payments. Visa isn't just buying a stake in a hot startup. It is positioning itself for a world where AI agents don't just write code, they spend money. Picture an agent that books your travel, restocks a company's inventory, or pays a software bill without a human clicking confirm. Every one of those actions needs a payment rail that can verify the agent is authorized, that it isn't being hijacked, and that the transaction can be trusted. That is a genuinely hard problem, and it is exactly the kind of plumbing Visa wants to own. The reason this matters beyond one investment is that it signals the money layer is starting to take agentic AI seriously. We have spent two years watching agents demo impressive tasks. The boring infrastructure, identity, authorization, payments, is what decides whether they ever leave the demo.

So watch this space. When the company that processes a huge share of the world's card transactions starts building for AI agents as customers, that is a bet that agents will soon be the ones holding the wallet.

## Now let's talk about the bill, because corporate America just got it. Two stories this week point at the same uncomfortable truth. The easy, exciting phase of enterprise AI is over, and the hard, expensive phase has begun.

The first is a wave of what people are calling AI sticker shock. Companies that rushed to roll out AI tools over the last two years are now looking at their actual costs, the per-token fees, the inference bills, the cloud compute, the consultants, and discovering the math doesn't always work. The pilot was cheap and dazzling. Deploying it to ten thousand employees, every day, at scale, turns out to be a very different number. The second story sharpens why. At TechCrunch Disrupt this week, a co-founder of Databricks made the point that enterprises are no longer asking whether AI is exciting. They have moved past that. The question now is whether it is safe to deploy broadly. And that is what actually kills deals. Not price alone, but the combination of cost and risk. Can we trust it with customer data, will it hallucinate something that gets us sued, can we explain its decisions to a regulator. Put those two stories together and you get the real state of enterprise AI in 2026. The technology works well enough to be tempting and not yet well enough to be safe at scale, and the bill for finding out is enormous. The companies winning right now aren't the ones with the flashiest demos. They are the ones who can prove their AI is governable, auditable, and worth the spend.

The hype cycle promised AI would slash costs. For a lot of companies, year one is the opposite, an investment with a fuzzy payoff. The ones who survive this phase will be the ones who treated AI like infrastructure, not magic.

## Which brings us neatly to a question every one of those companies should be asking. Can you actually trust what these models tell you? Two pieces of research this week suggest the answer is less than you would hope, and for stranger reasons than you would think.

The first is a sobering experiment. Someone took a thousand real-world fact-check claims, the kind of statements that get rated true or false by professional fact-checkers, and fed them to five frontier large language models, the top systems from the major labs. The result: the models disagreed with each other on sixty-seven percent of the claims. Two-thirds. These are the most advanced AI systems we have, and when you ask them to judge whether something is true, they reach the same verdict only about a third of the time. That should permanently end the fantasy that you can bolt one of these models onto a problem and treat its output as fact. Disagreement at that scale means most of them are wrong a lot of the time, and you often can't tell which one. The second study is almost comic, but it points at the same fragility. Researchers investigated whether being polite to a language model changes its accuracy. And it does. The phrasing of your prompt, whether you say please, how you frame the request, measurably shifts how often the model gets the answer right. Sit with that for a second. The correctness of the answer depends partly on your manners. That is not intelligence in any robust sense. It is a system exquisitely sensitive to surface patterns in language, which is wonderful for fluency and terrible for reliability.

The takeaway isn't that these tools are useless. They are remarkable. It is that they are confident and inconsistent at the same time, and those are exactly the conditions under which smart people get burned. Verify everything that matters.

## If today's models are this shaky, where does the next leap come from? That is the question driving the most ambitious, and most slippery, idea in AI right now: recursive self-improvement.

There is a new crop of labs that have essentially moved past AGI as their north star and replaced it with something they call RSI, recursive self-improvement. The dream is an AI that can improve itself, write better versions of its own code, design better training methods, and bootstrap its way toward superintelligence without waiting for humans. It is a seductive pitch, and it is also proving just as hard to pin down as AGI was. Because the moment you try to define recursive self-improvement precisely, it dissolves. Does a model fine-tuning a smaller model count? Does an AI suggesting a tweak to its own training loop count? Like AGI before it, RSI is becoming a phrase that means the thing just over the horizon, which is convenient if you are raising money and frustrating if you want to measure progress. Meanwhile, on the research fringe, there is a fascinating counter-current. A project making the rounds describes a kind of discovery machine that, instead of optimizing toward a goal the way today's AI does, explores the way nature does, wandering, branching, stumbling into solutions a goal-following system would never find. The argument is that our current AI is brilliant at climbing the exact hill it is pointed at, and hopeless at discovering there is a taller hill somewhere else entirely. That is a real limitation, and it is worth remembering whenever you hear that scaling alone will carry us to superintelligence.

So you have one camp betting that AI will improve itself into godhood, and another quietly pointing out that the architecture we have can't even explore properly yet. Both can't be right. My money says the interesting breakthroughs come from people questioning the recipe, not just pouring more compute into it.

## Speaking of compute, there is a quieter race happening underneath all of this. The hunt for the chips that actually run these models, and whether anyone can loosen the grip of the dominant player.

An investment firm called General Compute is making a pointed bet that the next breakout AI chipmaker will be SambaNova. The context here is that everyone is desperate for an alternative to the company that currently owns AI training hardware. We have seen a wave of challengers, Cerebras with its wafer-sized chips, Groq, and others, all trying to prove there is room for a second or third winner. SambaNova's pitch has long centered on efficient inference, running models faster and cheaper once they are already trained, and that matters enormously now that the bottleneck for most companies isn't building models, it is serving them affordably, which loops right back to the sticker shock we talked about earlier. Why does one investor's bet matter to you? Because the price and availability of AI compute sets the ceiling on everything else. If a real competitor emerges and inference gets dramatically cheaper, a lot of those enterprise deals that died on cost suddenly start to pencil out. The whole industry is, in a sense, waiting for the hardware to get cheaper so the software can finally make money.

Whether SambaNova specifically turns out to be the one, who knows. But the appetite for an alternative is enormous, and that much demand tends to eventually produce a supplier.

## Now to a fight that is going to define the rules of the AI era. Who owns the words these systems are trained on, and the answers they hand back out. CNN is suing Perplexity.

Perplexity, for the uninitiated, is an AI answer engine. Instead of giving you a list of links like a traditional search engine, it reads the web and writes you a direct answer, and it also makes an AI browser called Comet. CNN's lawsuit, filed in a New York court, makes two sharp accusations. First, that Perplexity's tools generate, in CNN's words, verbatim copies of its articles. Not summaries, not paraphrases, the actual text. And second, that Perplexity serves up information that is supposed to be locked behind CNN's paywall, while allegedly ignoring CNN's attempts to block its crawlers from scraping the site in the first place. The line in the complaint that cuts to the heart of it is this: human beings report, research, write, edit, and create the content that Perplexity takes without permission or compensation. That is the central tension of the AI economy in a single sentence. These systems are only as good as the human-made content they ingest, and the people making that content are increasingly unwilling to feed a machine that then competes with them for the same readers and never sends a cent back. Perplexity has been sued before, by other publishers, and it is far from the only AI company facing this. But the verbatim-copying claim is especially dangerous, because it is much harder to wave away as fair use than a loose summary would be.

However this case lands, it is part of a much bigger reckoning. The first wave of AI was built by treating the entire internet as free training data. The bill for that assumption is now coming due, one lawsuit at a time.

## Let's close with a quick round of stories worth knowing about.

First, Waymo is expanding again. Its newest robotaxi, a Zeekr-built minivan rebranded as the Ojai, is about to start carrying real passengers, not employees, in San Francisco, Los Angeles, and Phoenix. Rides start free while Waymo collects data, with paid trips to follow. The reason this matters is that Waymo's current Jaguar fleet runs on technology from 2020 and is reaching the end of its life. The Ojai is the platform Waymo plans to scale with, so this is less a new car and more the start of its next chapter. Second, for the open-source crowd, there is a Show HN project offering a full open-source workspace, mail, documents, spreadsheets, and drive storage, across web and iOS. Think of it as a self-hostable alternative to Google Workspace. In an era where every productivity app is racing to shove AI into your private data, the appeal of an open, ownable alternative only grows. And third, a small but telling one for developers. Ripgrep, the blazing-fast search tool that has become a staple in coding setups, including the kind that powers a lot of modern AI tooling, has published an explicit AI policy for contributions. As open-source projects get flooded with AI-generated pull requests, often low-quality and submitted by people who don't understand the code, maintainers are starting to set ground rules. It is a tiny document with a big signal. The open-source world is figuring out, in real time, how to coexist with a flood of machine-generated code.

Three different corners of tech, robotaxis, productivity software, and open source, all quietly rearranging themselves around a world reshaped by AI.
