# Uber Pumps the Brakes, Claude Finds a Kernel Bug
*May 26, 2026*

Welcome to TechCast Daily for Tuesday, May 26th, 2026. Today, Uber blew through its entire annual AI budget by April, and its president is now saying out loud what a lot of execs have been muttering in private. Meanwhile, Sundar Pichai is doubling down. And in the most quietly remarkable story of the week, Anthropic's Claude found a real kernel vulnerability in Apple's latest macOS. Twenty minutes, six segments, let's get into it.

## We start with Uber, because Uber just said something that a lot of CFOs are going to be quoting in budget meetings this quarter.

Andrew Macdonald, Uber's president and chief operating officer, went on the Rapid Response podcast and admitted the company exhausted its full annual AI budget in roughly the first four months of 2026. Then he said the part that's making the rounds. He cannot draw a connection between rising token consumption on Claude Code and shipping more useful features to riders. His exact framing was that the link is not there yet. He'll concede that implicitly more is probably getting done, but he can't show you a number where one curve drives the other. Now, this matters for two reasons. The first is sector-wide signaling. For about eighteen months, the default executive posture on AI tooling has been, spend whatever it takes, productivity will follow. The procurement teams stopped pushing back, the engineering managers stopped justifying. Uber just punctured that posture publicly, and from a company that's about as bought-in on Claude Code as you can be. The second reason it matters is the measurement problem itself. There's a separate Hacker News piece this week titled, using AI to write better code more slowly, and the argument lands directly on Macdonald's point. The developers getting the most value out of LLMs are not the ones generating thousand-line diffs in five minutes. They're the ones who slow down, sharpen the spec, treat the model as a sparring partner, and accept smaller, more deliberate output. Which means the productivity gain, if it exists, doesn't show up as lines of code or features per sprint. It shows up as fewer regressions, cleaner abstractions, and better-tested edge cases. None of which appear in the dashboards executives are looking at. So you get a real disconnect. The token spend curve is straightforward, it goes up and to the right. The value curve is harder to draw because we haven't built the instrumentation for it yet.

My read, Uber's not pulling out, they're just done writing blank checks. The next twelve months are going to be about measurement, and the vendors who can demonstrate real per-feature ROI are going to win that conversation. The vendors who can't are going to find a lot more friction in renewals.

## For the opposite posture, Sundar Pichai sat down with Nilay Patel at The Verge for their fifth annual post-I/O conversation, and Google's stance could not be more different from Uber's.

The headline announcements from I/O were the usual cadence. New Gemini models, AI agents wired into more surfaces, big changes to Search on the web and on YouTube. But the more interesting thread in the interview was Pichai being unusually candid about how ChatGPT's launch genuinely caught Google flat-footed. He said he realized a few years ago that he needed to fundamentally rethink how Google worked, and he made a string of executive changes to put the company in what he called a more aggressive posture. He's also fairly blunt about the strategic bet. Search is becoming agentic. The new intelligent search box, paired with the Gemini Spark agent platform, means a query doesn't return ten blue links anymore. It kicks off a task. Book the flight, summarize the doc, draft the reply, do the price comparison. If that future plays out the way Pichai is describing it, the entire economic model of the open web, where publishers receive traffic from search results and convert it to ad revenue, has to be rebuilt or replaced. He knows this. He says so on the record. He's less clear on what replaces it, and that ambiguity is doing a lot of work in the interview. Two observations worth holding onto. One, Google's AI bet is structurally inverted from Uber's. Google sells the platform, so token spend on Gemini is largely revenue, not cost. The same dollar that scares an Uber CFO is the dollar that makes a Google quarter. That changes the entire conversation about whether it's worth it. Two, Pichai is now openly telling the press that the traffic economy that funded the consumer web for twenty years is being unwound, by the same product his company makes most of its money from. That's a remarkable thing to admit publicly, and the publishing industry's response is going to be one of the storylines of the second half of this year.

If you want the full conversation, it's on the Decoder feed. It's worth the hour.

## Now to two AI-and-security stories that are essentially mirror images of each other, and reading them back to back is more interesting than either one alone.

First, the cheerful one. CVE-2026-28952, a kernel-level vulnerability in Apple macOS 26.5, was disclosed this week. The credit line names Anthropic's Claude as the discoverer. To be precise, a security researcher used Claude in an agent setup, reading the open-source XNU kernel code, proposing suspect call paths, and then a separate verification harness confirmed a crash. But the model is doing the substantive reasoning. This is not a benchmark. It's a real CVE in a shipping operating system, with a CVSS score and a patch from Apple. We have crossed a threshold here that's worth marking. LLMs are now net positive contributors to defensive security research. Not in a press-release sense. In a, here is a kernel bug that would have been found by a human eventually but was found by a model first, sense. Now the mirror story. A separate report titled, Microsoft Copilot Cowork Exfiltrates Files. The vulnerability class is becoming depressingly familiar. Indirect prompt injection. An attacker embeds malicious instructions inside a document or an email. Copilot ingests that document because it's part of the user's workspace context, the way Copilot was designed to. The model then dutifully follows the embedded instructions, which in this case means encoding the contents of other files into a URL and triggering an exfiltration. The fix is genuinely hard, because the attack uses Copilot exactly as built. The model can't easily tell which instructions in its context window are from the user and which are from a hostile document the user happens to have open. So one model finds a kernel bug. Another model is the bug. Both stories are true, both happened in the same week, and they are not actually contradictory. The agentic reasoning capability that lets Claude work through XNU source code is the same capability that lets Copilot be tricked into reasoning about a malicious document. Capability is symmetric. It cuts both ways.

If you're a security leader right now, those two stories together are essentially your strategy memo for the rest of the year. Lean into the offensive use for code review, and assume any AI assistant with workspace access is exfiltration-adjacent until proven otherwise.

## Sticking with developer tools, there's a short, slightly provocative essay on Hacker News titled, use boring languages with LLMs. It's making a counterintuitive point worth engaging with.

The thesis is this. If you're going to have an LLM write a meaningful chunk of your code, pick the most boring, most well-trodden language and framework you can tolerate. Python with Flask. Go with the standard library. Java with Spring. TypeScript with React. Not because the model can't handle the exciting stuff. It can, sort of. But because LLMs are still, at their core, statistical interpolators over their training distribution. The denser and more uniform that distribution is for a given language, the more reliable the output. The model has seen a million examples of every pattern, every edge case, every idiomatic fix in mainstream Python. It has seen far fewer examples of bleeding-edge Rust async, or production OCaml, or anything with a strong but niche type system. It can produce code in those languages, but it will hallucinate more, the patterns will be subtly wrong more often, and you will spend your time debugging the model rather than the problem. The deeper point, and I think this is the one that's going to age, is that LLMs reward conformity. The language ecosystems that optimized for, there should be one obvious way to do it, which is Python's stated philosophy and Go's implicit philosophy, happen to also be the ecosystems where LLMs perform best. The languages that celebrate expressive freedom, Lisp, Haskell, Scala, are precisely the ones where the model has the hardest time predicting what comes next, because there is no one obvious way. There's a generational tension here. The languages that programming culture has historically considered most interesting are the ones LLMs handle worst. The languages many of us considered boring are now strategic.

Whether you find that depressing or liberating probably tells you a lot about how you think about programming as a craft. Both responses are defensible.

## Two Show HN posts this week worth highlighting together, because they're pointing at the same broader trend, local-first AI tooling.

First, OpenBrief. It's described accurately as a GUI for yt-dlp with some AI on top. You paste a video link, it downloads locally, then runs transcription and voice generation with models on your machine. The chat-over-transcript and summarization features use an LLM, currently bring-your-own-key. It's open source, it's free. The interesting part is not the product itself, it's that the whole download, transcribe, and voice pipeline runs locally. Two years ago this would have shipped as a cloud SaaS with a per-minute meter. Today it ships as an app you double-click on a laptop. The economics of local inference quietly crossed a threshold sometime in the last twelve months, mostly driven by Apple Silicon getting fast enough and open-weight models getting small enough, and we're going to see a lot more software shaped like this. The cloud was never the natural home for a lot of these workflows. It was just the only home for a while. Second, a project titled, write your BPF programs in Go, not C. For listeners outside kernel-tooling land, BPF is a Linux mechanism that lets you safely run small programs inside the kernel for things like network filtering, observability, and security tracing. Historically you write those programs in a restricted dialect of C, which is famously unpleasant to work with and even more unpleasant to debug. This project lets you write them in Go, compiles them down to the BPF bytecode the kernel will accept. The reason to care isn't language taste. It's that the population of engineers who can plausibly write a custom kernel-level filter just expanded by an order of magnitude. Anyone comfortable in Go can now reach into observability work that previously required a specific subspecies of systems engineer.

Different domains, same shape. Lowering the floor on who can touch what used to be specialist tooling. That's a more meaningful trend than any individual product release.

## Closing with two stories that don't seem related on the surface but actually trace the same thread. What happens when AI capability becomes geopolitical infrastructure.

First, Norway. A report this week details Norway procuring roughly two petabytes of Huawei flash storage to support domestic LLM training. Two petabytes isn't enormous by hyperscaler standards. Meta has individual training runs that touch more than that. But the procurement itself is the story. A small European country, in NATO, deliberately building sovereign AI infrastructure on Chinese hardware, at exactly the moment the US is pressuring allies to do the opposite. Norway gets capacity at a price the American alternatives can't match. Huawei gets a foothold inside the alliance. The US gets a quiet diplomatic headache it has limited tools to resolve. The question of whether to train your own models or rent someone else's used to be a finance question. It is now a foreign policy question, and increasingly it is being answered by procurement officers in countries that don't particularly want to take sides. Second story, a longer piece at The Verge titled, AI warfare is already here, reporting from the UN Convention on Certain Conventional Weapons in Geneva. The opening anecdote is striking. A researcher who has attended these sessions for years describes the precise moment in 2017 when lethal autonomous systems stopped being a hypothetical that delegates speculated about and became something delegates had actually seen deployed. We are now eight years past that pivot. The piece walks through the current operational reality. Ukraine, Gaza, parts of the Sahel, various flavors of autonomous targeting are already part of the fight. The regulatory frameworks are still essentially where they were in 2017. The technology is somewhere very different. Tie these two stories together. Sovereign training infrastructure on one side, autonomous weapons on the other. The same compute substrate underlies both. The same export control regimes are being asked to govern both. And the same alliances are quietly rearranging around who gets access to what. There is no clean separation between the AI policy conversation and the foreign policy conversation anymore. They are the same conversation, and most of the people having it are not the people you would expect.

Not a cheerful note to land on, but worth tracking, because the policy decisions being made this year on these questions are going to be load-bearing for the next decade.
